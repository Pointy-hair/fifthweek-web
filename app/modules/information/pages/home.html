Removing the barriers to participation.

Everything published to a Fifthweek channel is ephemeral; it lasts only 30 days. This encourages participation by removing the permanent record, and focuses discussion on what you're doing now. It lets you price your channel lower, bringing more people on board.

Funding, simplified.

Forget about tiers and milestones, rewards and pledges. On Fifthweek you simply create a channel, set a weekly price, publish content, and let people subscribe. We believe a simpler platform means more subscribers, which means more funding for your work.

1. Create a channel

Create a channel and set a weekly price. Prices can start from just cents, letting you balance community size and affordability.

2. Publish your work

Start populating your channel with content. This could be your latest endeavors, previews of upcoming work, exclusive content, or requests for feedback.

3. Spread the word

Spread the word among your existing followers. We think you'll be surprised how many want to support you, and how many are interested in getting more of what you do.

Not quite what you're looking for? Try one of these…

We're not the only funding platform in town! Here are some great alternatives:

Kickstarter is the original crowdfunding website. Aimed more at one-off funding campaigns, they set the standard for everyone else.

Patreon specializes in recurring funding. They are structured similar to Kickstarter, with multiple tiers (we have multiple channels, which is similar), and milestone goals. They only take 5% commission, but pass payment processing fees on to the creator, which can become expensive if you're expecting smaller pledges.

Tapastic is a recurring funding platform aimed at the comic industry. It has lots of comic specific features, and is focused more on consuming the final product than nurturing ideas and getting feedback. They take a flat 50% (absorbing fees), and also have an ad revenue sharing program.

Pubslush is a crowdfunding platform aimed directly at authors and publishers, and one-off funding campaigns.

Indiegogo is similar to Kickstarter, but larger due to a more relaxed entry criteria. They are also aimed at one-off funding campaigns.
